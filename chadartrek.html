Walking on Ice: The Frozen World of the Chadar Trek

The Chadar Trek is not just a trek—it’s an expedition across one of nature’s most surreal winter landscapes. Set deep in the Zanskar region of Ladakh, this trek takes you over the frozen Zanskar River, forming a shimmering sheet of ice that locals call the Chadar (meaning “blanket” in Hindi). The journey typically begins from Chilling, a village about 60 km from Leh, and winds its way to Nerak, passing through towering canyon walls, icy caves, and remote hamlets that survive in extreme conditions. It's a once-in-a-lifetime experience where every crunch of your boot on the ice feels like a step into another world.
Rated as a difficult trek, Chadar demands both mental grit and physical endurance. With daytime temperatures often well below freezing and nights plunging to -20°C or lower, trekkers face real Himalayan challenges. But what makes it truly unforgettable isn’t just the terrain—it's the humbling experience of walking the same icy path used by locals for generations, especially when roads are cut off. You’ll share stories around campfires, sip butter tea to keep warm, and witness a stark beauty that exists only in deep winter. If you’re up for an adventure that tests your limits and rewards you with raw, untouched beauty, Chadar will stay with you forever.

Surviving the Ice: Safety Tips for the Chadar Trek

• Prepare for Extreme Cold:- Temperatures can drop below -25°C, so pack warm layers, thermal gloves, waterproof boots, and a good down jacket.
• Acclimatize in Leh:- Spend at least 2–3 days in Leh before the trek. It helps your body adjust to the high altitude and reduces the risk of Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS).
• Stay Hydrated, Even if You Don’t Feel Thirsty:- You might not feel thirsty, but dehydration is common in the cold.Sip water regularly and carry a thermos to keep it from freezing.
• Listen to Your Guide:- The ice can be tricky—listen to your trek leader, especially when walking on thin or cracking sections of the Chadar.
• Go with a Certified Trek Operator:- The Chadar Trek isn’t for solo adventures. Choose experienced, government-registered operators who provide backup support, oxygen, and trained guides.
• Don’t Ignore Early Symptoms:- Headaches, nausea, breathlessness, or dizziness should never be brushed off. Alert your guide immediately.

Culture & Cuisine

Though the Chadar Trek is all about braving nature’s extremes, the local culture adds warmth to the experience—literally and figuratively. The journey begins in Leh, where you’ll get a glimpse of Ladakhi life, shaped by centuries of living in one of the world’s toughest climates. The people here are incredibly resilient, deeply spiritual, and always ready with a smile and a cup of hot butter tea. Buddhist prayer flags, ancient monasteries, and the quiet strength of the locals create a sense of peace in this frozen land.
When it comes to food, simplicity and warmth rule. Expect hearty bowls of thukpa, momos, and tsampa —all designed to keep you fueled in the cold. Meals on the trek are usually basic but nourishing, and every warm bite feels like a blessing after a day on the ice. The culture here teaches you to slow down, respect the elements, and appreciate the simple joys of warmth and connection.

How to get there?

The Chadar Trek starts from Leh, Ladakh. In winter, air travel is the only way to reach Leh, as all roads are closed due to heavy snowfall. From Leh, trekkers head to the starting point near Chilling after acclimatizing for a couple of days.

BY AIR

Fly directly to Leh Airport from Delhi, Srinagar, or other major cities. Flights are regular in winter but may get delayed due to weather.

BY TRAIN

No direct trains to Leh. The closest station is Jammu Tawi (700 km), but it's not a practical option in winter.

BY CAR

Road access to Leh via Manali or Srinagar is closed in January–February. Driving to Leh is not possible during the trek season.
